The solar value problem:
cannibalization, curtailment & the declining capture ratio

Solar assets in CAISO are generating into a market they are reshaping — and the prices they earn are falling faster than the market average. This report traces that decline through three years of real market data and explains what it means for developers, owners, and traders.

01The capture ratio is collapsing

A solar asset doesn't earn the average market price — it earns a generation-weighted average of the hours it actually produces. As solar capacity has grown from 16.2GW to 23.5GW, those hours have become the cheapest in the day. The capture ratio has fallen 28.1 percentage points in two years.

02Why: the duck curve deepens every year

Solar doesn't generate when prices are highest — it generates when prices are lowest, and it's the reason prices are lowest. The midday price trough gets deeper each year as more capacity competes for the same hours. In 2023, solar earned $96.5/MWh at its peak generation hour. In 2025, the same hour paid $15.2/MWh.

03The next stage: negative prices and value-at-risk

A declining capture ratio is uncomfortable. Negative-price exposure is a crisis. In 2023 and 2024, no solar generation in January occurred during negative-price hours. In January 2025, 12.7% of all solar generation was dispatched into negative prices — roughly 1 in 8 MWh generated at a loss.

04Curtailment: the hidden revenue loss

Standard capture price only counts dispatched MWh. Curtailed solar represents real lost revenue — generation withheld during the same negative-price midday hours. January curtailment grew from 0.0 GWh (2023) to 115.0 GWh (2025) — a 1150.0x increase in two years.

05What this means for market participants

These trends compound each other. Lower capture ratios reduce revenues. Negative-price exposure adds direct losses. Curtailment adds hidden losses not in standard metrics. Together they describe structural deterioration in the economics of unhedged solar in CAISO — and a clear value signal for storage.

Key implications
1
Solar PPA pricing must account for declining capture. A PPA priced against average LMP will consistently overestimate revenues. The correct reference is the capture price — which is 57.8% below average in January 2025 and still falling.
2
Project finance stress tests need updating. The P10 daily capture ratio fell from 0.54x (2023) to 0.14x (2025). Debt service models built on 2022–2023 data are materially optimistic.
3
Curtailment is accelerating as a structural risk. From near-zero in 2023 to 115.0 GWh in January 2025 alone. As CAISO solar capacity approaches 30+ GW, curtailment during peak generation hours will become a persistent feature, not an occasional event.
4
The evening ramp is the value signal storage responds to. While solar earns $15.2/MWh at midday, the 17:00–19:00 PST ramp consistently prices at $56.3–$181.9/MWh. A 2-hour battery can charge at negative prices and discharge at the evening peak.
5
NP15 vs SP15 divergence is a real siting signal. NP15 held a 0.72x capture ratio in January 2025 vs SP15's 0.42x, with zero negative-price exposure vs 12.7%. Location matters.
